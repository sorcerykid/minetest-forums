Your conclusion does not make sense. The danger you outlined is that you use the work of someone else, violate some supposed right and get attacked for it. This can happen with normal copyright or any license if you violate the terms.
However, this is about someone else's copyrighted stuff. As for your own stuff, you don't have to worry. You cannot technically violate your own copyright or license. You have the right to do (basically) whatever with your stuff, nobody can sue you for your own work, no matter the license. Also, your story is awfully specific. You never must obey your own license. You are not more or less under a threat of copyright infringement for the license you choose for your own stuff.

The correct conclusion for a person who is paranoid about copyright would be to avoid publicly (re-)using other people's stuff with unclear permissions. That is, if you're paranoid at all, of course.

It is not a goal of the free software movement to force people to share all code ever written with the world. That would be quite draconian and anti-freedom, actually.

Finding loopholes is fun!
Here are some bad clauses that would still be perfectly fine under the current rule set (even considering the sentence you just quoted):

  • โYou may not use the mod in a schoolโ (a school is not a โgroup of peopleโ)
  • โYou may not use the mod if you are rubenwardyโ (=not a group of people)
  • โYou may not use the mod in a commercial mannerโ (a clause that can be interpreted very broadly to include redistribution on a single banner ad on a webpage. Monetized YouTube vids are also banned, of course)
  • โYou may not use the mod on a Mac computerโ
  • โYou may not use the mod for educational purposesโ
  • โYou may use the mod only for educational purposesโ
  • โYou may use the mod only if you post to 5 random people on Facebookโ
  • โYou may use Bucket Game only in Final Minetest, not in Old Minetest 5โ
  • โYou may redistribute the source code, but you must NOT read itโ xD
  • โYou may not try to reverse-engineer my obfuscated codeโ
  • โYou may not use the mod to make videos on YouTube, but Vimeo is OKโ
  • โYou may not depend on this mod / use its API without paying me a tiny, really insignificant royalty of only $1,000,000โ
  • โYou may not <insert all of the above>โ ;-)

I could go on forever, I think you get the idea. They are all some form of restrictions of a particular use or a blatant discrimination or demand something silly.

I consider all these restrictions to be bad and don't represent what Minetest stands for (quite the contrary!). Their effect is all the same: They all attack the user. For the distributor of the software, these clauses have no obvious benefit either. The distributor is not really harmed if one of these โviolationsโ happen. So any rhetoric of โI need to protect my own interestsโ does not really make much sense here. What happens is that the user is worse off, but the distributor is not better off. Such clauses only make sense if screwing over the user was the actual goal.

There's a very good reason why there is the point โDiscriminating against a field of endaveorโ in the Open Source Definition and the DFSG and the freedom 0 in the Free Software Definition. They exist precisely to weed out stupid restrictive clauses like those above.
